NDA vs Non-Compete Agreement: What's the Difference?

March 13, 2026 · 7 min read

p>

If you are hiring employees, working with contractors, or entering a business partnership, you have probably encountered both NDAs and non-compete agreements. While they are often mentioned together, these two contracts serve very different purposes.

What Is an NDA?

A non-disclosure agreement (NDA) is a legal contract that prevents one or both parties from sharing confidential information. Protected information typically includes trade secrets, business strategies, client lists, proprietary technology, and financial data. NDAs protect information, not restrict employment.

What Is a Non-Compete?

A non-compete agreement restricts a person from working for competitors or starting a competing business for a defined period within a specific geographic area after leaving a company. Non-competes restrict future employment or business activity.

Key Differences

NDA

  • Protects confidential information
  • Does not restrict employment
  • Can be mutual (both parties bound)
  • Enforceable in all 50 states
  • Used with employees, contractors, partners
  • Duration: 2-5 years typically

Non-Compete

  • Restricts future work activity
  • Limits where and when someone can work
  • Usually one-directional
  • Enforceability varies by state
  • Used with employees and business sellers
  • Duration: 6 months to 2 years

When Do You Need an NDA?

Use an NDA when sharing sensitive business information outside your core team: hiring freelancers who will see proprietary processes, pitching investors on product ideas, discussing partnerships, or onboarding employees with trade secret access.

When Do You Need a Non-Compete?

Non-competes make sense when an employee gaining competitive knowledge could cause harm by immediately joining a competitor: senior executives, salespeople with client relationships, or engineers with proprietary tech access.

Important: Non-compete laws vary by state. California, North Dakota, Oklahoma, and Minnesota have banned or restricted non-competes. Always check your state's laws.

Can You Use Both?

Yes. Many employment agreements include both. The NDA protects confidential information indefinitely while the non-compete prevents joining a direct competitor for a limited time. Using both provides layered protection for your business.
